# Your Money Game Plan: A Simple Guide for College Students

## The Basics - Words You'll See Everywhere

- **Capital** - Money (or anything valuable) you can use to make more money.
- **Assets** - Stuff you own that's worth something (cash, investments, property, etc.).
- **Investing** - Putting your money into things (like stocks) that can grow over time. It's riskier than a savings account, but the payoff is much bigger long-term.
- **Interest** - The fee someone pays to borrow money, OR the money you earn just for keeping your cash in certain accounts. It's usually shown as a percentage per year.
- **Compound Interest** - When you earn interest *on top of* interest you already earned. Over time this snowballs - the longer you leave it alone, the faster it grows. This is your biggest advantage as a young person.
- **Stocks** - Tiny pieces of ownership in a company. If the company does well, your piece becomes worth more. Some companies also pay you a little cash (called a dividend) just for owning their stock.
- **ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)** - A bundle of many stocks packaged together into one thing you can buy. Instead of picking one company, you own a little bit of hundreds (or thousands) of them at once. It's an easy, low-cost way to spread out your risk.

## Keeping Your Money Safe

- **Emergency Fund**
  - Cash you set aside *only* for surprises: car breaks down, medical bill, lost your job
  - The goal is 3–6 months of your basic expenses so you never have to use a credit card in a panic
- **HYSA (High-Yield Savings Account)** 
  - A savings account that actually pays you decent interest (around 4–5% right now) instead of the near-zero most regular banks offer
  - Your money is safe (FIC-backed/government-insured) and you can pull it out anytime
- **Regular Savings Account** 
  - The basic account at your bank
  - Keeps your money safe but earns almost nothing
  - A HYSA is better for anything you're parking for a while

## Retirement Accounts (Yes, Start Now)

- **401(k)** 
  - A retirement account your employer sets up for you
  - Money goes in *before* taxes (so your paycheck gets a little tax break), 
    and it grows without being taxed until you pull it out decades later
- **Employer Match** 
  - Free money
  - Your company puts extra cash into your 401(k) to match what you put in (up to a limit)
  - Always take this - it's an instant 100% return
- **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)** 
  - A retirement account *you* open yourself (not through a job)
  - It gives you tax benefits to help your savings grow faster
- **Roth IRA** 
  - The best option for most college students
  - You pay taxes on your money now (while your income is low and taxes are tiny), 
    then *everything* you pull out in retirement is 100% tax-free - including all the growth 
  - The younger you start, the more powerful this becomes

# The Actual Game Plan

## Goals

1. Set up a system that grows your money automatically (you shouldn't have to think about it)
2. Build enough wealth to be financially free by your mid-30s

## You Only Need Three Accounts

| # | Account | Why |
|---|---------|-----|
| 1 | **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)** | Your emergency fund lives here. Earns 4–5% safely. |
| 2 | **Roth IRA** | Your secret weapon. Pay low taxes now, grow money tax-free for 30+ years. |
| 3 | **Index Fund / ETF** | Own a piece of the entire U.S. economy in one purchase. Good options: VTI, VOO, or FSKAX. |

## Phase 1: College Years

### What You're Doing

- Building habits and protecting yourself - not trying to get rich overnight
- The main goal is to never end up using high-interest credit card debt because of an unexpected expense

### Emergency Fund

- **Put 10% of every paycheck into your HYSA**
- Example: Make $1,000 this month → $100 goes to savings
- Once you hit **$2,000–$3,000**, you can stop adding to it (that's plenty for a college student whose rent/tuition is already covered)

### Investing

- **Put 10–15% of your monthly income into your Roth IRA**
- Important rule: You can only contribute money you actually *earned* (job income, not gifts or loans)
- The yearly max is $7,000 or your total earnings - whichever is smaller

### Quick Budget Breakdown

| Monthly Income | Emergency Fund (10%) | Roth IRA (15%) | Left for Living & Fun (75%) |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **$500** | $50 | $75 | $375 |
| **$1,000** | $100 | $150 | $750 |
| **$2,000** | $200 | $300 | $1,500 |

## Phase 2: After Graduation (Career Years)

### What Changes

- Your income jumps - upgrade your savings system *immediately* before you get used to spending more

### Step-by-Step Priority

1. **Get the free money (401k match)**
   - If your company matches up to 4%, put in at least 4%. That's a guaranteed 100% return.
1. **Grow your emergency fund**
   - College-level savings won't cut it anymore
   - Build your HYSA up to **3–6 months of real expenses** (rent, insurance, food, utilities - probably $5,000–$10,000)
1. **Max out your Roth IRA**
   - Try to hit the $7,000/year limit. Automate monthly deposits so you don't have to think about it.
1. **FIRE - Financial Independence/Retire Early**
   - **Hit the 25–30% savings rate**
   - To retire early (the FIRE approach), you need to save/invest **25–30% of your income** - not just 10%.
   - The money you invest in your 20s does the heaviest lifting because it has the most time to compound

### Example Budget: $55,000/year salary (~$4,583/month gross)

| Category | % | Monthly $ | What It Covers |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Taxes** | ~22% | $1,008 | Federal, state, local taxes, healthcare |
| **Investing (FIRE)** | **25%** | **$1,146** | 401k match → Roth IRA → Index fund (in that order) |
| **Bills & Essentials** | ~35% | $1,604 | Rent, groceries, utilities, insurance, transportation |
| **Fun Money** | ~18% | $825 | Eating out, hobbies, travel, shopping - guilt-free |

### Pro Tips

- **Stop saving cash once your emergency fund is full** 
  - Redirect that money into investments instead
- **Spend your fun money without guilt.** 
  - If your investments and bills are automated first, whatever's left is yours to enjoy
- **When you get a raise, split it 50/50:** 
  - Half upgrades your lifestyle, half goes to investments
  - This keeps lifestyle inflation in check

## Credit Cards & Building Your Credit Score

### Why Credit Matters

- Your **credit score** is a number (300–850) that tells lenders how trustworthy you are with borrowed money
- A good score (700+) gets you lower interest rates on car loans, apartments, and eventually a mortgage - saving you tens of thousands of dollars over your lifetime
- You build credit by *using* credit responsibly over time - there's no shortcut

### The Rules of Responsible Credit Card Use

1. **Treat it like a debit card**
   - Never charge more than you can pay off *in full* this month
   - If you can't afford it with cash, you can't afford it on credit
1. **Pay your full statement balance every month**
   - This is the #1 rule - if you only pay the minimum, you get hit with 20–30% interest that erases any benefits
   - Set up autopay for the full balance so you never miss
1. **Keep your usage low**
   - Try to use less than 30% of your credit limit (under 10% is even better)
   - Example: If your limit is $1,000, keep your balance below $300 at any given time
   - This ratio is called "credit utilization" and it heavily impacts your score
1. **Never miss a payment**
   - Payment history is the single biggest factor in your credit score
   - Even one missed payment can drop your score significantly and stay on your record for 7 years
1. **Start with one card, keep it simple**
   - Get a student credit card or a secured card (you put down a deposit as collateral)
   - Use it for one small recurring expense (like a streaming subscription or gas) and autopay it
   - Don't open multiple cards at once - each application temporarily dings your score

### What to Look for in Your First Card

- **No annual fee** - you shouldn't pay money just to have the card
- **Student or secured card** - designed for people with no credit history
- **Cashback rewards** - even 1–2% back on purchases adds up over time
- Avoid cards with high fees, confusing reward programs, or "introductory 0% APR" traps that spike later

### Building Credit Over Time

- **Length of history matters** - keep your first card open even after you get better ones later
- **Be patient** - it takes 6+ months of activity to generate a score, and 2–3 years to build a solid one
- **Check your score for free** - most banks and apps (like Credit Karma) let you monitor it without hurting your score
- **Dispute errors** - check your credit report annually at [annualcreditreport.com](https://www.annualcreditreport.com) and dispute anything incorrect

### Common Traps to Avoid

- **Carrying a balance "to build credit"** - this is a myth. Paying in full every month builds credit just as well and costs you nothing in interest
- **Store credit cards** - they usually have terrible interest rates and low limits. Skip them
- **Cash advances** - using your credit card to withdraw cash from an ATM charges insane fees and interest immediately. Never do this
- **Cosigning for friends** - if they miss payments, *your* credit gets destroyed too

## Recommended Reading

- **The Simple Path to Wealth** by JL Collins - Written as letters from a dad to his daughter about investing. Super simple, focused on index funds. Perfect starting point.
- **I Will Teach You To Be Rich** by Ramit Sethi - Great for young adults. Covers automating your money, negotiating pay, and spending guilt-free (not boring budget spreadsheets).
- **The Bogleheads' Guide to the Three-Fund Portfolio** by Taylor Larimore - A short, easy read on building a simple investment portfolio that beats most complicated strategies.
- **Bogleheads Wiki** ([bogleheads.org](https://www.bogleheads.org/wiki/Main_Page)) - A community resource built on the philosophy of low-cost, long-term index investing.
